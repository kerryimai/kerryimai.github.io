Enumerable is a powerful module that gives you ways of working with arrays and hashes.It lets you do something to each or all of the items in an object. A lot of times in programing, you would have an object with large amount of data, and you would need enumerable to deal with them in order to not waste time repeating the same process again and again.

As you may have known, #map is one of the enumerables. And today's blog is going to help you understand this method better and throughly. #map functions the same as collect, and it is used more often than between the two. What does #map do that is so great? #map lets you operate a transformation to all items in a collection without altering the collection's original data. It returns you a copy of the altered version of the data.

Does that remind you of the method #each? Yes, all enumerable methods including #map are based off the method #each. Thus, what #map is doing is to accpet an invoke order and uses it on each of the items in a collection. However, unlike #each, #map returns you the altered collection and not just a single item in the collection. 

      

    Example:

    Your health concious sibling left a list of grocery
     items on the desk for your mother to buy later: 

    fruits = ['apple', 'banana', 'blueberry', 'peach']

    You dont want any of these items on the list but you are
    really craving for PIES!! So you make your version
    of her list and secretly swtiches the two: 

    fruitpies = fruits.map {|fruit| fruit + "pie"}

    print fruitpies  #=> ["applepie", "bananapie", "blueberrypie",
     "peachpie"]

    Now that looks much better! You are welcome =)
